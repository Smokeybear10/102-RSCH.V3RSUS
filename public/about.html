2.0

How it works.

from raw stats to a calibrated probability · in plain english
§ method

§ 2.1 — The stats Not head to head.

The most important thing to understand: when you select Khabib vs. McGregor, Khabib’s 5.3 takedowns per fight is his average across his entire career — against Iaquinta, Barboza, Johnson, everyone. Not specifically against McGregor.

So the model answers: “Given how each fighter performs across all opponents, what does the gap between their profiles predict?” It’s also what makes hypothetical matchups between fighters who never met possible.

§ 2.2 — The features It’s about the gap.

Instead of feeding raw stats for both fighters, we compute the difference for each stat. “Fighter A has 5.3 takedowns per fight, Fighter B has 2.1” becomes a single number: +3.2. This halves the data and directly encodes who has the edge on each dimension.

diff = Fighter 1’s stat − Fighter 2’s stat

Positive means Fighter 1 has the edge. Negative means Fighter 2. The pipeline produces 86 such features across striking, grappling, physical attributes, experience / momentum, and opponent quality.

§ 2.3 — The ensemble Three models, one vote.

Each algorithm has blind spots. Logistic regression misses complex interactions. Random forest can overfit to noise. Gradient boosting can be overconfident. By averaging all three — plus XGBoost and SVM in the extended v3.2 ensemble — we get a prediction that’s more stable than any single model. When they all agree, trust is high. When they diverge wildly, the fight is genuinely hard to call.

P(win) = (1 / 5) × Σi Pi(win | x)

§ 2.4 — The pipeline Anatomy of a prediction.

When you hit Run, five things happen in sequence:

  1. Look up each fighter’s most recent career-wide stats from the dataset.
  2. Subtract Fighter 2’s stats from Fighter 1’s across all 86 features to get a difference profile.
  3. Scale each gap relative to the training data so all features sit on the same playing field.
  4. Five models independently return a win probability. Their mean becomes the final prediction.
  5. The explanation engine decomposes the result into SHAP attributions, category-level edges, and nearest neighbors.

§ 2.5 — The explanation Why, in three layers.

Every prediction comes with three layers of reasoning so you can judge it yourself:

  • Key factors. We decompose the ensemble mean into per-feature contributions (SHAP values). Each stat gap gets a score showing how much it pushed the prediction toward one fighter, ranked with both fighters’ raw values shown.
  • Edge analysis. We group all features into four domains (striking, grappling, physical, experience) and average each group’s contribution into a 0–100 score. 50 = dead even. Above 55 strongly favors Fighter 1. Below 45 strongly favors Fighter 2.
  • Model consensus. Each classifier’s vote is shown individually. If they line up (66%, 63%, 61%) the prediction is stable. If they diverge (70%, 45%, 55%) the fight is genuinely unpredictable and the result deserves a grain of salt.

§ 2.6 — The honest bit How accurate is it, really?

68.4% on held-out 2022–2024 fights (n=1,284). For context: a random guess sits at 50%. Always picking the red corner hits 54%. Vegas closing lines sit at roughly 65–70%, with access to camp reports, injury intel, and weight-cut whispers we don’t have. Academic ML studies on UFC consistently land in the 58–68% range.

MMA is chaotic. One punch can end any fight regardless of stats. 68% means the stats explain more than half the story. The rest is heart, timing, and luck.
