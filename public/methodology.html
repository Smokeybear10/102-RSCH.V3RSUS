Technical Deep Dive

Methodology

How the ensemble model works—from raw data to prediction to explanation.

01

The Data

Career Stats, Not Head-to-Head

This is the most important thing to understand. When you select Khabib vs. McGregor, Khabib's "5.3 takedowns per fight" is his average across his entire career—against Iaquinta, Barboza, Johnson, everyone. Not specifically against McGregor.

This means the model answers: "Given how each fighter performs across all opponents, what does the gap between their profiles predict?" It's also what makes hypothetical matchups between fighters who never fought each other possible.

What the Model Sees

  • Sig. strikes landed per minute
  • Career takedown accuracy
  • Win/loss record and streaks
  • Height, reach, weight, age
  • Title bout experience

What It Doesn't See

  • Performance against this specific opponent
  • Training camp quality
  • Injuries or weight cut issues
  • Stylistic game-planning
  • In-fight momentum or crowd
Example

Select Israel Adesanya vs. Alex Pereira. Adesanya's stats come from his most recent fight (maybe vs. Du Plessis), averaged across ~25 career bouts. Pereira's come from his latest, across ~12 UFC fights. The model computes the gaps—Adesanya has more rounds fought, Pereira has higher strike accuracy—and pattern-matches against thousands of similar stat profiles.

02

Feature Engineering

Differential Features: It's About the Gap

Instead of feeding raw stats for both fighters, we compute the difference for each stat. "Fighter A has 5.3 takedowns/fight, Fighter B has 2.1" becomes a single number: +3.2. This cuts our data in half and directly encodes who has the edge on each dimension.

diff = Fighter 1's stat − Fighter 2's stat

Positive means Fighter 1 has the edge. Negative means Fighter 2. Simple.

Why We Excluded Betting Odds

The dataset includes Vegas odds, but we deliberately threw them out. Odds are set by humans who already assessed the fight—using them would mean our model is partially just predicting what bookmakers already predicted. More importantly, odds don't exist for hypothetical matchups, so including them would break the system for the most interesting use case.

03

The Models

Three different algorithms, each with their own strengths, vote together on every prediction.

LR

Logistic Regression

Linear Model

The "explainer." Treats the prediction as a weighted sum of all stat gaps. Simple, stable, and—crucially—we can decompose its output to show exactly which stats drove the prediction and by how much.

~59.7% accuracy
RF

Random Forest

100 Decision Trees

The "pattern finder." 100 decision trees each look at random subsets of the data and features. Together they catch non-linear patterns—like reach advantage mattering more for volume strikers—that a straight line can't capture.

~60.5% accuracy
GB

Gradient Boosting

Sequential Learner

The "corrector." Builds trees one by one, each focusing on fights the previous trees got wrong. It's like studying your mistakes—each round gets a little better at the hard-to-predict matchups.

~59.3% accuracy

Why Three Models Instead of One?

Each model has blind spots. Logistic Regression misses complex interactions. Random Forest can overfit to noise. Gradient Boosting can be overconfident. By averaging all three, we get a prediction that's more stable and reliable than any single model. When all three agree, you can trust the prediction more. When they disagree, that's a signal the fight is genuinely hard to call.

Final Probability = (LR + Random Forest + Gradient Boosting) / 3
04

How a Prediction Happens

1
Look Up Fighters
Find each fighter's most recent appearance in the dataset and pull their career-wide stats.
2
Compute the Gaps
Subtract Fighter 2's stats from Fighter 1's across all 35 features to get a "difference profile."
3
Standardize
Scale each gap relative to the training data so all features are on the same playing field.
4
Three Models Vote
Each model independently produces a win probability. Their average becomes the final prediction.
5
Explain the Why
The explanation engine breaks down which stat gaps mattered most, which categories favor whom, and shows past matchup history.
05

The Explanation Engine

Every prediction comes with three layers of reasoning so you can judge it yourself.

1

Key Factors

We decompose the Logistic Regression's output into per-feature contributions. Each stat gap gets a score showing how much it pushed the prediction toward one fighter. The top 8 most impactful factors are shown with both fighters' raw values so you can see for yourself.

2

Edge Analysis

We group all 35 features into Striking, Grappling, Physical, and Experience, then average each group's contribution into a 0–100 edge score. 50 = dead even. Above 50 favors Fighter 1. Below 50 favors Fighter 2. A quick snapshot of who has the edge and where.

3

Model Consensus

See how each of the three models voted individually. If all three agree (60%, 62%, 58%), the prediction is solid. If they diverge wildly (70%, 45%, 55%), the fight is genuinely unpredictable and you should take the result with a grain of salt.

06

How Accurate Is It?

About 60%—and here's why that's solid

Each model is tested using 5-fold cross-validation: the data is split 5 ways, and each slice takes a turn as the test set. No model ever sees its test data during training.

Vegas has training camp reports, injury intel, and insider knowledge we don't. Academic ML studies on UFC consistently land in the 58–68% range. The reality: MMA is chaotic. One punch can end any fight regardless of stats. 60% accuracy means the stats explain more than half the story—the rest is heart, timing, and luck.
