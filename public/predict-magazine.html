The Machinery

On Methods

Behind every prediction on this page sits a consensus of five independent minds. An XGBoost model, a LightGBM, a logistic regression, a random forest, and a kernelised support-vector machine each look at the same eighty-six engineered features and each return a probability. We take the mean. We calibrate it with Platt scaling. We publish it.

Features are the boring part of the story and the most important part of it. Career-weighted strike rates, takedown accuracy, sub attempts per fifteen minutes, reach, age, and a dozen shapes of recent-form momentum. Everything on a five-year exponentially decayed average, so last Saturday counts more than 2014.

What the model will not tell you is whether the fighter slept poorly, cut weight badly, or trained the right gameplan. It sees only what the record sheet sees. But on twenty-three years of records, that is already a great deal.

The Deck
What the interactive tool returns
A calibrated win probability with a 95% bootstrap interval. Per-model consensus across all five classifiers. SHAP attributions ranking the top six factors that drove this specific call. A category-level edge score across striking, grappling, physical, and experience. Calibration context showing how the model has performed on fights in your prediction's confidence bucket. And, where they exist, the nearest historical matchups by cosine similarity.
A Note On Vegas
Where this model falls short
Closing lines sit at roughly 65–70% accuracy. They see our feature set and more: camp intel, injury whispers, weight-cut quality. Our 68.4% keeps up with the book for middle-bucket fights, but is quietly outgunned at the tails. We say so loudly.

On Consensus
Agreement is the signal
Every one of the five classifiers calls this one for Pereira, though they disagree on the size of the edge. The SVM is most confident (67/33); the logistic regression least (61/39). When all five agree on direction, the ensemble historically hits 74% accuracy. When they split 3–2, 55%.
